Hitler Youth: Growing Up in Hitler's Shadow

A Historical Examination of Nazi Germany's Youth Organization

Introduction

The Hitler Youth is a generation of young people who devoted their energy and passion to the Hitler Youth organization, leaving a mark on world history. They were proud to be in the Hitler Youth. Formed in 1926, it offered its members excitement, adventure, and new heroes to worship.

Hitler's Vision

"I begin with the young. We older ones are used up… But my magnificent youngsters! Look at these men and boys! What material! With them, I can create a new world."

—Adolf Hitler

Hitler counted on Germany's boys and girls. On January 30, 1933, Adolf Hitler was appointed Chancellor of Germany, largely because of the Hitler Youth's efforts.

Growth and Membership

By 1939, all other youth organizations were banned. The Hitler Youth (boys) and the League of German Girls (girls) became almost mandatory. Over 82% of eligible youth participated. Jewish youths were not allowed to join and set up their own groups in the 1930s.

Membership Growth

  • End of 1933: 100,000 members
  • 1937: 5.4 million members
  • 1940: 7.2 million members

The group grew rapidly due to state encouragement and peer pressure. In March 1939, membership for ages 10-18 became mandatory. Non-compliance could result in legal punishment.

Daily Activities

Boys

  • Military Drills
  • Weapons Training
  • Farming
  • Sports (mainly boxing)

Girls

  • Gymnastics
  • Home cooking
  • Sewing
  • Nursing

War Effort

Hitler Youth served as soldiers, nurses, and secretaries. They prepared care packages to support the war effort.

Youth in illegal youth groups could be arrested or sent to concentration camps.

Indoctrination

"I belonged to Adolf Hitler, body and soul"

—Alfons Heck, former Hitler Youth Member

Hitler Youth was designed to indoctrinate Hitler's ideology into the next generation. After World War II, it took former members years to step back from the indoctrination.

Banning scouting sent the message of obeying or being punished, which also drove more children to join.

Resistance

Some boys who refused to join created illegal groups. The Edelweiss Pirates, containing around 5,000 members, were one such group. They:

  • Attacked Hitler Youth members
  • Worked to sabotage their activities
  • Scribbled anti-war graffiti on walls
  • Participated in resistance

End of the War

In 1945, younger boys were pulled to the front lines. These inexperienced children were essentially recruited for suicide missions. Children who refused could be executed.

After the war, the Hitler Youth was disbanded.
