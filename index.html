Hitler Youth History Project

West Delaware 4th hour History

Introduction

The Hitler Youth was a group for young people in Nazi Germany. It started in the 1920s and became a big, official organization in the 1930s. The group gave kids activities and a sense of belonging, but it also taught Nazi beliefs and prepared many members to help during the war.

Hitler's Vision

"I begin with the young... With them, I can create a new world."

—Adolf Hitler

Hitler saw the young people as the future of his ideas and used the Hitler Youth to try to shape their thoughts.

Origins & Timeline

  • Early 1920s: small local Nazi youth groups formed.
  • 1926: groups began to come together under the name "Hitler Youth".
  • 1933: after the Nazis took power, independent youth groups were closed and the HJ grew quickly.
  • 1936: laws made the organisation more official and standardized.
  • March 1939: membership became effectively required for many youths (about ages 10–18).
  • 1944–1945: older teens were increasingly used in the war, sometimes in fighting roles.
  • 1945: after Germany's defeat, Allied authorities banned Nazi groups, including the Hitler Youth.

Sources:Britannica.

Leadership & Organisation

Key leaders and how the group was organised:

  • Baldur von Schirach — led the Hitler Youth from 1931 to 1940.
  • Arthur Axmann — took over in 1940 and led during most of the war.
  • Structure: national leaders at the top, then regional (Gau) leaders, then local units. There were also special branches for aviation and naval training.
  • The Hitler Youth answered to the Nazi Party and later worked more with the army for training and wartime tasks.

Sources: Bundesarchiv.

Growth and Membership

By 1939, other youth groups had been banned and the Hitler Youth (for boys) and the League of German Girls (BDM) were the main youth organizations. The Nazis reported high membership numbers, but pressure and laws pushed many to join.

Official membership numbers

  • End of 1933: ~100,000 members
  • 1937: ~5.4 million
  • 1940: ~7.2 million

Structure & Age Groups

The organisation had different groups by age and gender:

  • Deutsches Jungvolk (DJ): boys 10–14.
  • Hitlerjugend (HJ): boys 14–18.
  • Jungmädel: girls 10–14 (part of the BDM).
  • Bund Deutscher Mädel (BDM): girls 14–18; training focused on fitness, home skills, and preparing for motherhood.
  • There were also special units for training in flying, sailing, or job skills linked to wartime needs.

Sources: Bundesarchiv

Daily Activities

Boys

  • Physical training and drills
  • Basic map, compass, and sometimes weapons training (age-dependent)
  • Camping, hiking, and sports (mainly boxing)
  • Work training for farms or factories during the war

Girls

  • Physical education and gymnastics
  • Cooking and sewing
  • Basic nursing and health skills
  • Lessons on motherhood and family roles

Recruitment & Propaganda Methods

The Nazis used schools, media, events, and social pressure to get kids to join:

  • Teachers and school activities pushed students toward the Hitler Youth.
  • Rallies, radio shows, and films made membership look exciting.
  • Uniforms, badges, camps, and competitions made kids want to belong.
  • Legal rules and the closing of other groups left few options for those who didn't want to join.

Sources: USHMM & Britannica.

Indoctrination

"I belonged to Adolf Hitler, body and soul."

—Alfons Heck, former Hitler Youth member

The Hitler Youth mixed physical training with lessons in Nazi ideas. Younger kids learned stories and slogans, while older teens got more political lessons and military training.

Sources: Alfons Heck & USHMM

War Effort

As the war went on, Hitler Youth members helped in many ways

  • They worked as helpers during air raids and on anti-aircraft teams.
  • They helped in factories, postal services, and other support roles.
  • In 1944–1945, many older teens were pushed into combat or local militia units because Germany needed more soldiers.

Sources: USHMM

Wartime Roles & Crimes

Most members did support work, but some were involved in actions that helped deportations or violence in occupied areas. Late in the war, Hitler Youth groups fought on the front and had many losses.

Sources: USHMM.

Coercion, Discipline & Consequences

  • After membership became required, refusing to join could cost a student opportunities at school or work and could lead to legal trouble.
  • Inside the Hitler Youth, discipline was strict. Members could be punished or reported for any disobedience.
  • Families could also be attacked by a young person's refusal to join.

Sources: Bundesarchiv & USHMM.

Exclusions & Persecution

Jews, Romani people, people with disabilities, and political opponents were not allowed to join. Some excluded youths formed their own groups or tried to leave Germany, but options were limited and got worse over time.

Sources: USHMM; Bundesarchiv.

Resistance & Regional Variations

Groups like the Edelweiss Pirates pushed back culturally or sometimes helped others. Resistance varied by city and region. People who resisted could face arrest, prison, or worse.

Sources: USHMM

Aftermath & Denazification

After Germany lost the war, the Allies banned Nazi groups and tried to remove Nazi ideas from schools and society. Former members went through re-education programs and communities had to deal with the long-term effects of having raised many young people under Nazi influence.

Sources: USHMM.
